  • Donizetti's "La Favorita"

    Gaetano Donizetti's "La Favorita" tells precisely of a long and complex publishing history characterized by multiple editions, translations, cuts and staging, profoundly different from each other and signed by an alternation of different librettists, starting with the first French edition "La favorita," first staged in Paris in 1840 and translated into Italian by Fausto Broussard for the publisher "Ricordi":
    by librettists Alphonse Royer, Gustave Vaëz and Eugene Scribe, it was presented as a reinterpretation of the "Ange de Nisida," commissioned the year before by Donizetti himself, characterized by the first changes in places, eras and names; in fact, the story was rewritten around the love triangle between the historical characters of Alfonso XI of Castile, his "favorite" Leonora of Guzman and the novice friar Fernando.

    The 1842 second edition edited by librettist Francesco Jannetti is the first Italian translation, and features a number of radical changes contextual to linguistic, audience and Catholic censorship demands that Donizetti disapproved of, starting with the unofficial title "Leonora di Guzman" chosen for the Padua premiere: in addition to italianizing the names, replacing the French Alexandrines with Italian loose verse and merging or splitting scenes, Jannetti lightened and "secularized" some situations and characters from the French original, retouching many verses, especially in the final bars, to make them consistent with the content.

    In the following years, "La Favorita" was the subject of further translations and revisions, such as the one commissioned in 1843 by La Scala in Milan to librettist Callisto Bassi, who set it in Syria in 1113 with the title "Elda" and with the curtain falling immediately after Leonora's death, while in other contexts Jannetti's ending continued to be used.
    In 1860, however, a version by Giuseppe Cencetti entitled "Daila" was staged in Rome, set among the Ottomans in the 14th century, with a mixed text of the previous two.

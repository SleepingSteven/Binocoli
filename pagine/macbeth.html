  • Verdi's "Macbeth"

    Giuseppe Verdi's "Macbeth" features two versions that offer not only differences in style and musical forms, but also different psychological balances and different dramaturgical perspectives that are difficult to reconcile. By the work of Francesco Maria Piave, one of Verdi's trusted librettists, the libretto also feature the faithful adaptation by librettist Andrea Maffei of the Shakespearean tragedy of the same name:
    the first version staged in Florence in 1847, in fact, concludes with a tragic and peremptory tone respecting the tones of the original opera.

    In 1865, however, Verdi and Piave worked to stage a new French version in Paris that would be in line with the tastes of audiences beyond the Alps and that would allow the inclusion of dance scenes: to do so, it was necessary to change considerably the tone of the opera, and consequently the finale itself, which then became characterized by scenes of collective exultation by the men of Macduff's army.

    In 1874, a translation of the French edition was first presented at La Scala in Milan, establishing itself as the definitive edition.
